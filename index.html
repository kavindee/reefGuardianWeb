Literature Survey

Coral reef support an estimated 25% of all marine species.[1]

Figure 1. Coral reef habitats around Sri Lanka [1]

Coral reefs, vital for maintaining oceanic ecosystem balance, face unprecedented challenges demanding innovative conservation strategies, especially in biodiversity-rich regions like Sri Lanka. The fusion of machine learning (ML) and mobile technologies, particularly the application of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) like ResNet-50, has marked a transformative era in coral reef monitoring [2]. Sri Lanka’s diverse coral species, numbering 171 across 65 genera, underscores the ecological richness and the pressing need for preservation [3]. Unregulated coral exploitation and environmental threats necessitate automated systems for effective monitoring, leveraging deep learning methods, particularly CNNs fine-tuned on diverse datasets [4].

To address the worldwide coral bleaching crisis, remote sensing technology emerges as a valuable tool. Our research explores spectral analysis approaches differentiating between healthy and bleached corals, highlighting a significance of remote sensing for coral health assessment [5]. This aligns with the urgency emphasized in recent research regarding the degradation of coral reefs due to climate change, pollution, and illegal activities [6].

Research Gap

Following areas are the research gaps found in most of the recent researches.

Identification & classification

There are no records of a smart solution for coral identification and classification in Sri Lanka. Symptoms of coral disorders show similar characteristic appearances, making it a challenge to provide a solution for identifying and classifying healthy and non-healthy corals.

Severity assessment

A smart solution for the identification of healthy and non-healthy corals is reported in some regions, but the assessment of the severity of disease conditions and the progression level of coral damage has not been attempted.

Information sharing

A real-time communication system to speed up information sharing between divers, marine biologists, and researchers has been identified as a priority need for effective coral identification and health assessment.

Research Problem & Solution

Proposed Problem

How can we develop a smart application to accurately classify coral and coral diseases, and provide real-time surveillance and information sharing to stakeholders for effective coral reef conservation in Sri Lanka?

Coral bleaching and algae infestation are the most threatening issues facing coral reefs in Sri Lanka. Due to coral bleaching, extensive areas of coral reefs have been severely damaged, and an estimated large number of corals need to be identified for conservation efforts. An economical loss to the marine ecosystem and tourism industry will result if more than 30% of coral cover is lost due to COTS infestation. For efficient management of coral bleaching and COTS, early identification and effective communication among divers, marine biologists, and conservation professionals are needed.


Product Demonstration - Solution

Proposed Solution

CoralCare utilizes mobile and web-based software to manage coral bleaching and Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (COTS) infestation, which devastate coral reefs. In cases of coral bleaching, the whitening of corals, often associated with environmental stressors, makes it difficult to distinguish affected corals accurately. Similarly, the damaged appearance of corals due to COTS infestation is difficult to distinguish from other forms of coral degradation. Therefore, Deep Learning techniques like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) for feature extraction will be used to identify coral conditions accurately. Divers, marine biologists, researchers, conservation officers, and the general public will be able to identify coral health accurately by capturing a photograph of the coral with symptoms. The location will be extracted by Geo Tags in order to gather anonymous data using OpenWeatherMapAPI for future predictions. The severity of coral bleaching is calculated based on multiple symptoms using CNN. Since COTS are associated with the damaged corals, counts of starfish are taken using OpenCV / YOLO object detection while the infected area is calculated using MASK R-CNN. Using the above-mentioned parameters, the progression level is determined. Real-time notifications are sent whenever an affected coral is found. Details of the affected corals, location, and severity records enable researchers and conservation officers to take remedial actions. Infected areas and danger zones that might be affected in the future can be visualized through Google mapping technologies. Nearby users are notified to take precautions to protect nearby coral reefs before they are affected.

Research Objectives

Coral Identification and Classification

This objective involves developing a system to accurately identify and classify different coral species. Using advanced image processing and machine learning techniques, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), the application will analyze photographs of corals to determine their species and health status. This will help in monitoring the diversity and distribution of coral reefs in Sri Lanka.

Solution for Coral Health Maintainance

The application will provide solutions for maintaining and improving coral health. By analyzing environmental data and coral condition reports, the system will suggest best practices for coral care, recommend interventions for stressed or diseased corals, and provide guidelines for mitigating factors such as pollution and climate change. This objective aims to support proactive and informed conservation efforts.

Coral Quality Measuring

This feature will assess the quality and health of corals using image analysis and machine learning algorithms. The application will measure parameters such as bleaching extent, presence of pests like Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (COTS), and overall coral vitality. Tools like OpenCV and MASK R-CNN will be used to quantify the extent of damage or health, providing precise metrics for researchers and conservationists.

Crowd Sourcing and Information Sharing

To enhance data collection and community involvement, the application will incorporate crowd sourcing features. Divers, tourists, and local communities can upload coral images and observations, contributing valuable data to the research project. Real-time information sharing and notifications will be facilitated to keep stakeholders informed about coral health status and conservation actions. This collaborative approach will enable a broader and more dynamic dataset, enhancing the overall effectiveness of the conservation efforts.

Methodology

Figure 2. Overall System Diagram.

The proposed pest and disease Surveillance system consists of 4 main components. They are;

  1. Coral identification and classification
  2. Solution for coral health maintainance
  3. Coral quality measuring
  4. Crowdsourcing and information sharing.

The system diagram outlines a comprehensive process for identifying and classifying coral health using mobile and web-based software. Here's a detailed description of the system components and workflow: Firstly, users capture an image of the coral using their mobile device, along with the GPS coordinates of the location where the image was taken. This information is then uploaded to the system as a screenshot or evidence. The system employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for health detection. The uploaded image undergoes preprocessing to enhance its quality and extract relevant features for analysis. The CNN analyzes these features to classify the coral as healthy or suffering from conditions such as bleaching or other diseases. The results from the image processing and quality measurement stages are stored in a database. This data is then analyzed to identify trends and make future predictions using tools like OpenWeatherMapAPI. This helps in understanding the broader impact and potential future risks to coral reefs. Real-time notifications are sent to relevant authorities whenever an infected coral is detected. Users receive the analysis results on their mobile devices, including the health status of the coral and any necessary precautions to be taken. This ensures timely interventions and helps in mitigating the spread of coral diseases.

Finally, the system uses Google mapping technologies to visualize infected areas and potential danger zones. This allows conservation professionals and the general public to see where coral reefs are most at risk and take proactive measures to protect nearby coral ecosystems. This visualization aids in strategic planning and resource allocation for coral conservation efforts.
